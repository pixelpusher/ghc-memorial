Family Histories from War Memorials

2014 marks the centenary of the start of World War One, the war many said would be over by Christmas. But, by the end of 1914 trenches ran from the Belgian coast to the Swiss border and by 1915, the war had reached a bloody stalemate. After four long years of heavy fighting, just under a million British men were dead, over two million were wounded and over 15,000 civilians had been killed.

Following the war, a grief stricken nation struggled to come to terms with the war and mourn its dead. The repatriation of the war dead was impossible; there were just too many. Without a funeral to attend or a grave in the UK to visit, families and communities needed somewhere to focus their grief. From the 1920s, grieving communities began campaigning for permanent war memorials in their local communities.

Memorial Committees were formed whereby people from the same village, urban borough, work place or school came together to raise funds, design and erect local war memorials.
